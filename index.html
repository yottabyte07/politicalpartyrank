People-First Index — Framework & Rankings
The People-First Index is an independent dimension introduced in this report to evaluate parties on criteria that are often absent from purely electoral analyses. The index measures the degree to which a party's ideology, funding sources, policy record, and public positions align with the interests of ordinary citizens — as opposed to corporate benefactors, resource extractors, or transient electoral incentives. A party that advocates free education as a structural right scores higher than one that announces cash transfers before elections. A party that leads protests against illegal sand mining scores higher than one whose state governments have documented sand mafia links. A party that received zero corporate bond funding scores higher than one that encashed hundreds of crores from raided companies.

Six sub-metrics constitute this index: Anti-Corporate Capture (20%), Natural Resource Protection (18%), Fundamental Rights Approach (20%), Farmer and Labour First (17%), Ecological Sustainability (15%), and Rights-vs-Freebies (10%). Each is scored out of its maximum weight and aggregated to a score out of 100.

Corporate Funding and People-First Alignment

The electoral bond data released by the ECI following the Supreme Court's February 2024 ruling reveals a clear inverse relationship between corporate bond receipts and people-first policy positions. Parties that received the largest volumes of corporate funding — BJP (₹6,060 cr), BRS (₹1,806 cr), TMC (₹1,609 cr), and INC (₹1,422 cr) — each show weaker scores on the Anti-Corporate Capture dimension. Parties with zero recorded bond receipts — including NTK, CPI(M), JMM, NPF, AAP, and NPP — correspondingly score higher on this sub-metric. The Supreme Court explicitly noted that the scheme created conditions for quid-pro-quo arrangements between political parties and corporations.

Rights-Based vs Freebie-Based Welfare

A central distinction in this index is between structural entitlements and pre-election transfers. Structural welfare — such as the Right to Education Act (INC, 2009), NTK's manifesto proposal to make free education from primary to PhD a constitutional right, or CPI(M)'s public health infrastructure in Kerala — embeds rights into law and administration. By contrast, cash transfers and goods distributed weeks before election schedules, with no institutional framework, constitute electoral inducements. Parties whose welfare commitments are predominantly of the structural variety score higher on the Rights vs Freebies sub-metric regardless of whether they are in power or in opposition.

Natural Resource Stewardship

India's sand mafia problem — documented across Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, and Karnataka — consistently implicates governing parties. Research shows that sand mafias operate through networks involving politicians, bureaucrats, and enforcement agencies. Parties whose state governments have documented sand mafia links, or that enabled large-scale river mining under their tenure, score lower on Natural Resource Protection. Parties that have led active public protests against sand mining, opposed highway projects on ecological grounds, or passed legislative resolutions against deep-sea mining privatisation score higher.

Emerging Parties and the Governance Penalty

Parties that have never held legislative seats face an inherent disadvantage in the Overall Rankings because governance delivery (18% weight) requires a track record of implementation. However, the People-First Index is intentionally designed to be agnostic to incumbency — it rewards ideological consistency, funding integrity, and policy positioning irrespective of whether a party has governed. This design choice allows the index to fairly evaluate parties like NTK, which has a 15-year record of consistent ideological positions and protest-based advocacy, even in the absence of electoral victories.

Overall Party Rankings — All Metrics Combined
The overall score aggregates eleven weighted metrics. Governance and Policy delivery carries the highest weight (18%) and directly disadvantages parties that have not held power. The People-First Index contributes 10% to the overall score. Criminal record percentage and party-hopping are penalty metrics. No party is scored to a predetermined outcome — all scores derive from verifiable public data.

Electoral Bond Receipts by Party — 2019 to 2024
Data sourced from the Election Commission of India's disclosure following the Supreme Court's February 2024 order declaring the Electoral Bond Scheme unconstitutional. The court found the scheme "violative of voters' right to information" and noted it created conditions for quid-pro-quo arrangements. Of the total ₹12,769 crore encashed, 96.91% came from corporate entities. Eight of the ten largest bond recipients were ruling parties in at least one state at the time of receipt. Parties listed at zero received no recorded electoral bond funding during the scheme's operation.

Pattern: Raids, Donations, and Case Closures

Research published in Humanities and Social Sciences Communications (Nature, 2025) identified three documented corruption patterns in the electoral bond data: companies donating to the ruling party after being raided by central agencies; companies receiving government contracts shortly before or after large donations; and newly incorporated companies making substantial donations without commensurate declared revenues. BJP, as the central governing party with control over investigative agencies, appears most frequently in these patterns, though similar dynamics are documented for state ruling parties including BRS, TMC, and BJD.

Parties with Zero Bond Receipts

The following recognized parties have no recorded electoral bond funding: Naam Tamilar Katchi (NTK), CPI(M), Jharkhand Mukti Morcha (JMM), Naga People's Front (NPF), Aam Aadmi Party (AAP), National People's Party (NPP), Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP), Rashtriya Janata Dal (RJD), Janata Dal (Secular), Shiromani Akali Dal, AIMIM, and Samajwadi Party. Zero receipts may reflect ideological rejection of the scheme, lack of corporate interest in funding smaller parties, or both. NTK's 2026 manifesto explicitly supports the Supreme Court's ruling against electoral bonds as a matter of party policy.

Methodology and Scoring Framework
This report uses a weighted multi-metric scoring model applied uniformly to all ECI-recognized parties. Scores are derived from publicly available data. No party was assigned a score to achieve a predetermined ranking outcome. Where data is limited (particularly for smaller regional parties), scores reflect the best available information and are noted conservatively.

Key Scoring Rules

  • Criminal record score = 10 × (1 − declared_criminal_pct / 100). Source: ADR India affidavit analysis.
  • Electoral bond receipts are deducted from the Anti-Corporate sub-metric on a logarithmic scale.
  • Multiple documented party switches reduce No Hopping score to 2–3/10. Zero alliances = 9.5/10.
  • Parties with no governance record score 2.5/10 on Governance (18% weight), materially capping their overall total.
  • Rights-based welfare (embedded in legislation) scores 8–9/10 on Rights vs Freebies. Cash transfers before elections score 4–5/10.
  • Sand mining or resource plunder allegations during a party's tenure reduce Nature Protection scores.
